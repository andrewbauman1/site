People hear "technical PM" and assume one of two things. A burned-out developer who drifted into project management, or the designated translator who nods along in standups and repeats things back in plain English for everyone else.

Neither one is quite me.

My background is in information systems, technical enough to read a schema, business-minded enough to sit across from a CFO and hold my own. So when I write Python or pull data with SQL, I'm not reaching for a skill I never had. I'm using the one I came in with.

Here's what this actually looks like day to day: a client asks for something that sounds small, a report tweak, a data cleanup task, the kind of thing that should take an afternoon. Before I take it anywhere, I go look at the data myself. *What tables does this touch? What joins does it need? Is the "simple" version even structurally possible, or does it quietly depend on three other things nobody mentioned?*

Running the query is the easy part, honestly. The harder (and most important) part, is understanding *why* the client wants it. *What decision does this feed? What does it need to look like on their end?* That's what lets me go back to my team with something they can actually build from, instead of a vague paraphrase of what someone in a Zoom call said forty minutes ago.

Skip that step and implementation work very quickly turns into a game of telephone. Client says X. PM repeats it to engineering. Engineering pushes back: *"It's more complicated than that."* PM relays that back to the client a little flatter than before. Nobody's cutting corners. The request just loses a little accuracy at every hop, and a day or two disappears chasing down what got lost.

I'm not going to tell you every PM should know how to write Python fluently. That's not the point I'm trying to make.

The point is what the skill protects. Engineering time doesn't get spent building the wrong thing. Clients get a straight answer instead of a few rounds of "let me check and get back to you." It's not a line for a resume. It's just fewer places for a project to quietly go sideways.
